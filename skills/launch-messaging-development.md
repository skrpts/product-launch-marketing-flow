## Capability

Develops the complete messaging architecture for a product launch, from high-level positioning through audience-specific value propositions, proof points, and messaging guidelines. Produces a messaging framework that ensures consistency across all launch channels and assets.

## When to Use

- Planning a new product launch (any tier)
- Repositioning an existing product for a new market or audience
- Developing messaging for a major feature release
- Refreshing launch messaging that is not resonating post-launch
- Creating the messaging foundation before handing off to copywriters and designers

## Process

1. **Situation Analysis** — Assess the launch context:
   - What is the product and what does it do?
   - Who is the target audience and what problem does the product solve for them?
   - What is the competitive landscape? Who else addresses this problem?
   - What is the market timing? Is this a new category, an established market, or a niche?
   - What unique advantages does the product have? (features, technology, price, brand, ecosystem)

2. **Positioning Framework** — Develop the core positioning using the standard framework:
   - **For** [target audience] **who** [have this need/problem]
   - **[Product]** is a **[category]** that **[key benefit]**
   - **Unlike** [competitive alternative], **[product]** [key differentiator]

   Test the positioning against three criteria:
   - Is it true? (Can you substantiate the claims?)
   - Is it relevant? (Does the target audience care?)
   - Is it differentiated? (Does it separate you from competitors?)

3. **Messaging Pillar Development** — Define 3-4 messaging pillars that support the positioning. Each pillar should be:
   - A distinct benefit theme (not a feature)
   - Supported by at least 2 proof points (data, testimonials, demonstrations)
   - Relevant to a specific audience pain point or aspiration
   - Defensible against competitive claims

4. **Audience Messaging Adaptation** — Adapt the core messaging for each target audience segment:
   - What does this audience care about most?
   - Which messaging pillar resonates strongest with them?
   - What language, tone, and level of detail do they expect?
   - What objections will they have, and how does the messaging preempt them?

5. **Messaging Hierarchy** — Establish a clear hierarchy:
   - **Headline message:** The single most important thing to communicate (10 words or fewer)
   - **Elevator pitch:** The complete value proposition in 30 seconds (50-75 words)
   - **Full narrative:** The complete launch story with context, problem, solution, proof, and call to action (200-300 words)
   - **Proof points:** Specific evidence supporting each messaging pillar

6. **Messaging Guidelines** — Document how the messaging should be used:
   - Approved language and terminology
   - Language to avoid (competitor names, unsubstantiated claims, jargon)
   - Tone and voice guidelines specific to the launch
   - Messaging do's and don'ts for each channel

## Inputs

- Product brief (features, benefits, target audience, pricing)
- Customer research (pain points, language, decision criteria)
- Competitive analysis (competitor positioning, claims, weaknesses)
- Company brand guidelines (voice, tone, existing messaging)
- Launch goals (awareness, adoption, revenue targets)

## Outputs

- Positioning statement
- 3-4 messaging pillars with proof points
- Audience-specific messaging adaptations
- Messaging hierarchy (headline, elevator pitch, full narrative)
- Messaging guidelines for channel execution
- Objection handling framework

## Constraints

- Never write messaging that the product cannot substantiate — aspirational claims erode trust
- Test all messaging against competitive claims to ensure differentiation is real, not just perceived
- Avoid insider language — use the words customers use, not internal product terminology
- Positioning must be singular — a product that tries to be everything to everyone has no positioning at all
